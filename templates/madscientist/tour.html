Take a Tour

Want to get right to it? You can see a public board (you can't edit or add any ideas or groups, but you can see it in action) here.

MadScientist revolves around three concepts: groups (or lists), categories (or facets), and tags. Although you can use them in any way that makes sense to you, I find it is useful to think about how you plan to use the store of fragments that you're saving.

The application is designed to make it easy to label ideas quickly and consistently—once you have a basic system. You don't have to figure out everything in advance, but it pays to spend some time thinking about recording and retrieval. Why are you recording it? What are you planning to use it for? What are some useful ways to classify the data, considering your purpose? How finely do you want to segment it?

Groups

Groups are broad groups of ideas, usually related to your purpose for collecting them. For example, I have a group called "Music Wishlist" where I jot down songs I hear on the radio that I'm thinking about purchasing, and another group called "Create Me" where I store ideas for arts and crafts I want to make. So it's clear when I'm in the mood to buy some music I can just check my Music Wishlist group, and so on. In MadScientist, each one appears as its own tab (see tab bar below).

When you go to write down an idea, it's best if there's one and only one obvious group to put it in. Otherwise how will you be able to stumble upon it later without wasting time looking through a bunch of irrelevant groups? If it's confusing, how do you know you'll always make the same decision of where to put it?

Tags and Categories (or Facets)

Categories are a way to group related tags together and are intended to represent a particular aspect, or facet, that universally applies to a group of ideas. A category (or facet) should refer to an objective, intrinsic quality, so that it is immediately clear what kinds of tags belong under each category. For example, in a group devoted to ideas for craft projects, two useful categories are "Medium" and "Skill" (see the picture below for an example).

When the categories are chosen correctly, tags can be applied unambiguously and consistently with little thought. The point is not to exhaustively tag every idea with everything you can think of: the point is to be able to quickly identify and tag the qualities about the idea that will help you find it later. So in the case of craft projects, I can immediately single out all sewing project ideas for browsing.

If you're confused about the whole idea of faceting and not sure where to start, try reading this great article on faceted classification.
